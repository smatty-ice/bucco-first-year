# Fun Facts, Cool Research, and Things That Are Just Neat

A grab-bag of things that are interesting in their own right, that you can share with Nina or anyone who asks about the new dog, and that reframe what an animal is and what Bucco specifically is capable of.

---

## Cognition

**Dogs read human pointing better than chimpanzees do.** Brian Hare's foundational work at Duke showed dogs follow human gaze and pointing gestures fluently — chimps mostly don't. This is one of the few cognitive tasks where dogs outperform our closest primate relatives, and it's a product of domestication (wolves don't read pointing well either).

**Dogs have functional theory of mind, sort of.** They distinguish whether a human is looking at them or not before deciding whether to "steal" food. They behave differently in front of an attentive human than an inattentive one.

**Dogs experience genuine separation distress, neurologically.** fMRI work (Berns et al. at Emory) on awake, unrestrained dogs showed activation in the caudate nucleus (reward area) when the dog's primary human appeared — the same pattern that lights up when humans see their own children.

**Chaser the BC learned 1,022 nouns.** Not 1,022 commands. Different nouns for 1,022 different objects, each one a unique referent. And she figured out new ones by exclusion — if you ask for a word she didn't know and there's one unfamiliar object in front of her, she'd retrieve that one.

**Bunny the Sheepadoodle, via AAC buttons, has produced multi-button sentences that suggest grammatical sequencing.** The science on AAC dogs is still being adjudicated, but the methodology (push-button word boards) at minimum shows dogs can chain words intentionally. (Border Collie / Poodle mix.)

**Rico the German Shepherd was the first dog studied scientifically for vocabulary.** ~200 words. His work led to Pilley taking on Chaser, who went 5× further.

**Dogs dream.** REM sleep behavior in dogs maps reliably to dreaming. Puppies dream much more (longer REM stretches, more visible twitching/vocalizing) because they have more new experiences to consolidate.

## Sleep

**Puppies need 18-20 hours of sleep a day.** Not "could use" — *need*. Most "hyperactive puppy" complaints trace to insufficient sleep, not insufficient exercise.

**Adult dogs sleep ~12-14 hours/day.** Distributed across short bouts rather than one long block.

**Dogs have two distinct REM/NREM cycles, like humans.** But cycle length is shorter (~20 min vs. 90 min in humans), which is why they nap and wake more frequently.

**Puppy REM at 12 weeks is ~30% of total sleep.** Adult REM is ~10%. The drop happens slowly over the first 6 months and correlates with the consolidation of new behaviors.

## Border Collie Specifics

**Old Hemp** (born 1893, Northumberland) is the foundation of nearly every modern BC pedigree. He was famous for a quiet, intense working style that became the breed standard for stock-handling.

**The BC didn't get AKC recognition until 1995** — over a century after Old Hemp — because working breeders resisted, correctly anticipating that show breeding would split the population into working and non-working lines.

**"Strong eye" vs. "loose eye"** is a real distinction in working BC genetics. Strong-eyed dogs work with intense, low-stalking pressure. Loose-eyed dogs work more upright and may use voice. Both styles work; different stock and handlers prefer different.

**A herding BC can move sheep without ever touching them.** The "eye" is so threatening to sheep (in prey-prediction terms — it looks like a stalking predator about to chase) that the sheep move under nothing but visual pressure.

**BCs were ranked the #1 most trainable dog breed by Coren (1994).** The metric ("obedience to first command in <5 reps") is crude, but the result has held up across different methodologies.

**Striker the BC opened a non-electric car window in 11.34 seconds** (Guinness World Record, 2004). He learned by watching his owner.

**Chaser was a working-line BC**, not show-line. The research subject pool for "BCs that learn 1000+ words" comes disproportionately from working lines because they have the focus and drive to sustain three years of daily training.

**The BC herding instinct usually shows around 6-12 months.** Pups that don't show it at 4 months haven't failed — the wiring isn't mature yet.

## Sensory and Physical

**Dogs see in color, but limited.** Two-cone color vision (blue and yellow) — similar to red-green colorblind humans. A red ball on green grass is harder for them to see than a blue one.

**Dogs see ~60 frames per second of motion**; humans see ~24. Old CRT TVs flickered for dogs the way fluorescent lights flicker for migraine-prone humans. This is part of why BCs in particular are sensitive to old TVs and screens.

**Dogs have ~220 million olfactory receptors**; humans have ~5 million. The olfactory bulb in a dog brain occupies a proportionally massive area. They aren't living in our world; they're living in a richer chemical world we can barely perceive.

**A dog's sense of smell can detect a single drop of blood in an Olympic-sized swimming pool.** Forensic and medical detection dogs work at limits that defy conventional analytical chemistry.

**Dogs can smell time.** Specifically, they can detect the rate of odor decay, which is why a dog "knows" you're coming home around 5pm — your morning scent is at a specific decay state at that time.

**Border Collie heart rate at rest: ~70-100 BPM**, lower in trained working dogs.

**Dogs can develop calluses on their elbows from hard floors.** The "elbow callus" is normal and not painful, but it's a marker that the dog is consistently lying on hard surfaces — consider providing softer bedding.

## Behavior

**Yawning is contagious between humans and dogs**, with dogs more likely to yawn after their primary human yawns than after a stranger. Possible empathy marker; research is ongoing.

**Tail wags aren't just "happy."** Wag direction and amplitude carry information.
- More to the right = positive emotional state (left-brain dominance)
- More to the left = negative/avoidance state (right-brain dominance)
- Low, slow wag = uncertain
- Helicopter wag with whole-body wiggle = full positive arousal

**The "play bow"** is a meta-signal — it tells the other dog (or human) "what I do next is play, not for real." Dogs maintain this signal throughout play and it's the single most reliable indicator that they're enjoying themselves.

**Dogs whose tails are docked or naturally bobbed have measurable difficulty communicating with other dogs.** Tail-based signals carry significant social information and dogs without functional tails are at a communication disadvantage.

**BCs are statistically more likely than most breeds to engage in displacement behaviors** (yawning, lip-licking, scratching) when conflicted. Watch for these — they're early stress indicators before the dog escalates to growling/snapping.

## Training Science

**Marker training (clicker) was invented for dolphins in the 1960s** by trainers including Karen Pryor. Dolphins can't be physically reached for treat delivery, so trainers used a whistle marker to indicate "what you just did is correct" — followed by a fish. Pryor adapted the method for dogs in the 1980s.

**A study comparing positive-reinforcement and aversive-method training found**: dogs trained with aversive methods showed elevated cortisol for hours after sessions, more displacement behaviors during training, and *worse* performance on novel tasks. Same handlers, same dogs, randomly assigned method.

**The 50% acceleration figure for marker training** comes from comparative studies on complex behavior acquisition (Smith & Davis, IAABC Foundation Journal). For simple behaviors the advantage is smaller.

**A "jackpot" reinforcement** (unexpectedly large reward) has been shown to *temporarily increase* the probability of the immediately-preceding behavior much more than steady reinforcement. Use sparingly to mark breakthrough moments.

## Domestication

**Dogs split from wolves between 20,000-40,000 years ago.** The exact date is contested; recent ancient-DNA work suggests two domestication events (one in Europe, one in East Asia) that later merged.

**The "fox domestication experiment"** (Dmitri Belyaev, Russia, started 1959) selectively bred silver foxes for tameness alone. Within 10-20 generations, the foxes showed dog-like traits: floppy ears, curled tails, piebald coats, juvenile faces. This established that "domestication syndrome" is genetically linked — selecting for one trait (tameness) brings the others along.

**Dogs and wolves share 99.96% of their DNA.** The differences are concentrated in starch digestion genes, social cognition genes, and some neurodevelopmental timing genes.

**Dogs are the only animal that reliably forms cross-species social attachments with humans during a single sensitive period of development.** Cats can, but with much more variability. Most other species have to be hand-raised from neonatal stage and even then bond weakly.

## Health and Longevity

**Border Collies have a typical lifespan of 12-15 years.** Working lines often live longer than show lines, possibly because of leaner body condition and lower cancer rates in less-inbred populations.

**The Purina lean-dog study** (followed Labrador pairs over 14 years; one dog of each pair fed normally, the other 25% less) found lean dogs lived **~1.8 years longer on average** than their ad-libitum littermates. This is one of the cleanest pieces of evidence in canine health research.

**Hip dysplasia is multifactorial, not just genetic.** Genetics sets the susceptibility; environment determines expression. The single biggest controllable factor in growth: avoiding overfeeding and excessive impact (stairs, jumping off furniture) until growth plates close at ~12-18 months.

**Spay/neuter timing has measurable effects on cancer risk and orthopedic disease.** The 2020 UC Davis study covering 35 breeds found, for medium and large breeds, that pre-pubertal sterilization increased rates of: cranial cruciate rupture, hip dysplasia, lymphoma, hemangiosarcoma, mast cell tumors, and osteosarcoma. The effect was breed-specific and often striking (golden retrievers spayed before 6 months had ~4× the rate of joint disorders).

## Bucco-Specific Curiosities

**The name "Bucco" is the Italian word for "fop" or "dandy"** — also a Pittsburgh Pirates nickname (from "Buccaneers"). Pittsburgh dog, Pirates dog, fitting on multiple levels.

**Bucco's brain is currently ~70-80% of its adult mass.** It will reach ~95% of adult size by 6 months but continue myelinating internal connections until ~18-24 months.

**Bucco's primary socialization window closes around June 8, 2026** (week 16). What he experiences positively between today and then will shape him more than any other 4-week window of his life.

**Bucco's 16-week vaccine appointment (June 11)** is also a training session. The vet building is now a stimulus that will form a classical association — strongly positive or mildly negative depending on the next ~6 visits. Bring treats. Lots of them.

**Adult Bucco's predicted weight: 35-50 lb** based on parental size and current growth trajectory. Working-line BCs typically run lean at 35-45 lb. Bring your best guess to the vet for accurate medication dosing.

**Bucco was raised with cats from day one.** This is unusual and valuable. Most BCs raised without cats develop persistent stalking interest. Bucco's social template will include cats as conspecifics in some sense — he's likely to be safer around them than the average BC, *as long as* the leashed-around-cats protocol holds through adolescence.

## The Last One

**Dogs are the first species humans deliberately co-evolved with.** Everything else came later — livestock, cats, horses. The relationship between humans and dogs is the foundational case study for inter-species cooperation. When you sit on the floor and teach Bucco "sit" using kibble and a marker word, you're participating in a 20,000-40,000 year old behavioral exchange.

That's pretty cool.
