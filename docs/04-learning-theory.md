# Learning Theory — The Real Science Behind Susan Garrett's Method

The Garrett framework isn't a brand. It's a clean application of behavioral and learning science assembled by someone who's spent four decades testing it in agility, the most consequence-dense dog sport that exists. This document unpacks the science under each piece — why high rate of reinforcement works, why "no" doesn't, why classical conditioning has to come first.

---

## 1. The Four Building Blocks

All animal learning sits on four mechanisms. Anyone training a dog is using these whether they know it or not.

### 1.1 Habituation

Reduced response to a repeated stimulus that turns out to be neither rewarding nor harmful. The doorbell rings 100 times with nothing scary attached → eventually the puppy stops reacting.

**Bucco implication:** "ignore the doorbell" gets trained by feeding kibble through 50 reps of the doorbell ringing while nothing important happens. Not by yelling "QUIET."

### 1.2 Sensitization

*Increased* response to a repeated stimulus that's been paired with negative outcomes. The doorbell rings → the resident dog bursts in barking → the puppy associates doorbell with chaos → over time the puppy reacts harder to the doorbell, not softer.

**Bucco implication:** habituation requires neutral repetitions. If the puppy is reacting and you're not managing it, you're sensitizing him, not habituating him.

### 1.3 Classical Conditioning (Pavlov, 1903)

A neutral stimulus paired repeatedly with a meaningful stimulus takes on the meaning. Bell + food → bell alone produces salivation.

For Bucco, every pairing is doing this:
- Crate + food + sleep + calm = crate is good → he runs in willingly
- Crate + isolation + crying = crate is bad → he resists the crate

**The biology:** the amygdala (emotion) and hippocampus (memory of stimuli) form a fast, durable link. Once a strong classical association is formed, it can be modified but not erased.

### 1.4 Operant Conditioning (Skinner, 1938)

Behavior is shaped by its consequences. Behaviors that produce rewards increase; behaviors that produce nothing (or punishment) decrease.

**The biology:** the basal ganglia and dopamine system reinforce action-outcome pairings. Dopamine isn't released when the reward arrives — it's released in anticipation, when the cue or behavior predicts reward. This is why the *expectation* of reward is what drives behavior.

## 2. The Operant Quadrants — And Why Garrett Only Uses One

| Stimulus | Behavior increases | Behavior decreases |
|---|---|---|
| **Added** | **+R** (positive reinforcement) | +P (positive punishment) |
| **Removed** | -R (negative reinforcement) | -P (negative punishment) |

- **+R:** add a treat after sit → sit increases. THE QUADRANT.
- **+P:** leash pop after pulling → pulling decreases. Damages the relationship; creates fear of the handler.
- **-R:** ear-pinch until dog picks up dumbbell → dog learns to pick up dumbbell to make the pinch stop. Used in some traditional methods. Cruel and unnecessary.
- **-P:** turn away when dog jumps → jumping decreases. Useful adjunct to +R for some attention-seeking behaviors. NOT a primary tool.

### Why +R-only?

1. **Neurochemistry.** +R produces dopamine. +P and -R produce cortisol. Cortisol disrupts learning. Dopamine accelerates it.
2. **Relationship.** The handler becomes a predictor of good things, not bad. This is the relationship that holds in adolescence.
3. **Generalization.** +R-trained behaviors transfer between contexts better. +P-trained behaviors collapse outside the original context (because they're context-bound — the dog learned "stop *here* or it hurts," not "this is wrong").
4. **No collateral damage.** +P can suppress one behavior and cause five unrelated fears or aggressions to emerge. Documented across studies; see Ziv 2017 review in *Journal of Veterinary Behavior*.

## 3. Reinforcement Schedules — Why VR Wins

### Continuous (CRF)

Every correct response is reinforced. **Use for:** teaching a new behavior. **Drawback:** weak resistance to extinction. Stop reinforcing and the behavior fades fast.

### Fixed Ratio (FR)

Every Nth correct response is reinforced. **Use for:** maintenance. **Drawback:** "post-reinforcement pause" — the dog learns the pattern and slows down right after a reward.

### Fixed Interval (FI)

Reinforcement available every N seconds, regardless of how many responses. Produces a "scallop" pattern — responding peaks just before each interval ends.

### Variable Ratio (VR)

Reinforcement on average every Nth response, but unpredictable. **The strongest schedule.** Highest response rate. Most resistant to extinction. This is the slot-machine schedule.

### Variable Interval (VI)

Reinforcement on average every N seconds, but unpredictable. Moderate response rate; very durable behavior.

### For Bucco — practical schedule progression:

```
Stage 1 (teaching):    CRF — every rep gets food
Stage 2 (early fluency): CRF still, but small chunks of variable food (1 piece vs 3)
Stage 3 (fluency):     VR-2, then VR-3 (random pattern)
Stage 4 (maintenance): VR-5 to VR-10, plus environmental reinforcers (Premack)
Stage 5 (life): Occasional jackpot reinforcement; mostly environmental
```

**Never go to a fixed schedule.** It creates pauses. Always keep some variability.

## 4. Rate of Reinforcement (RoR) — The Single Most Underrated Variable

When teaching a new behavior, Garrett's guidance is **15-30 reinforcements per minute.** Three to five seconds between markers.

Why this matters:

- **Attention is preserved by frequency.** If reinforcement is rare, the dog disengages between deliveries. If frequent, the dog stays plugged in.
- **Working memory window is short.** The puppy must connect the behavior to the reward; large gaps make this fail.
- **Dopamine system stays engaged.** A high pulse rate keeps the predictive expectation high.

Compare:
- **Low RoR (2-3/min):** "Sit." (wait) "Good boy." (wait) "Stay." (wait) "OK release!" → boring, low engagement, slow learning.
- **High RoR (20+/min):** Capture sit → marker → food → next rep within 3 seconds → marker → food → marker → food → end on a win → walk away. Three minutes of this produces more learning than 30 minutes of low-RoR work.

## 5. Shaping — Building Behaviors That Don't Exist Yet

You can't reinforce a behavior that doesn't happen. So you reinforce *approximations* and gradually raise the bar.

**Example: shaping a "spin" cue from nothing.**

1. Dog looks slightly left → mark + treat
2. Dog turns head left → mark + treat (you stopped reinforcing the head look)
3. Dog moves left front paw → mark + treat
4. Dog rotates body 1/8 turn left → mark + treat
5. Dog rotates 1/4 turn → mark + treat
6. Dog rotates 1/2 turn → mark + treat
7. Dog completes full spin → mark + treat
8. Add the verbal cue "spin"

Each step is a *successive approximation* of the final behavior. The skill of the trainer: break the behavior into small enough steps that the dog succeeds at each one. Too big a jump → dog fails → frustration → quits.

### Common shaping behaviors for Bucco

- **Hand target:** reinforce looking at the hand → sniffing the hand → nose-touching the hand.
- **Settle on mat:** reinforce walking near the mat → stepping on it → lying on it → staying for 1 sec → 2 sec → etc.
- **Crate Games:** look at crate → step toward it → step in → all four feet in → turn around → lie down → stay 1 sec → etc.

### Errorless learning

Garrett emphasis: set the environment so the puppy succeeds. Don't lure for too many reps (he becomes lure-dependent). Don't ask too much too soon (he fails and learns frustration). When a session goes badly, the failure is on the trainer for setting too big a step.

## 6. Markers — The Bridging Stimulus

Karen Pryor's clicker work, originating in dolphin training in the 1960s, formalized the marker. A short, distinct sound delivered the instant the correct behavior happens, followed by food within 1-2 seconds.

### Why the marker matters

- The behavior-to-reward gap is the learning bottleneck. By the time food appears, the dog may have moved, scratched, looked away.
- The marker fires within 0.3 seconds of the behavior, anchoring *exactly* what is being reinforced.
- Pavlovian conditioning makes the marker itself rewarding (paired enough times with food, the marker produces a mini-dopamine pulse on its own).

### Timing precision

- Within **0.5 sec** of the behavior: ideal
- Within **1.5 sec**: still effective
- Beyond **2 sec**: association weakens; you may be reinforcing whatever the dog did at the 2-sec mark

### Clicker vs. verbal "yes!"

| | Clicker | Verbal "yes!" |
|---|---|---|
| Precision | Identical every time | Varies with mood |
| Availability | Need to be holding it | Always available |
| Distinctness | Stands out from environment | Can blend with other talk |
| Best for | Detail work, shaping, sport | Pet dog daily training |

**For Bucco:** verbal is fine. Pick one short, distinct word (one syllable, sharp consonant). Don't dilute it by using it casually. The marker should mean *food is coming, right now*.

## 7. The Premack Principle — Using Life as Reinforcement

David Premack, 1959-1965: a higher-probability behavior reinforces a lower-probability one.

Translation for dogs: **a thing the dog wants to do can be the reward for doing a thing you want.**

| Reward (high-probability) | Requirement (low-probability) |
|---|---|
| Sniffing | Loose leash to the bush |
| Greeting a person | Sit politely first |
| Going outside | Wait at the door |
| Eating dinner | Settle on mat |
| Tug game restarts | "Drop" |
| Off-leash freedom | Reliable recall on the long line |

### Why it's underused

Owners default to food because it's easy. But food gets boring; food gets satiated. Environmental rewards never satiate — the dog *always* wants to sniff that bush.

### For Bucco — apply Premack everywhere:

- Don't open the front door until he's sitting.
- Don't put down the food bowl until he's calm.
- Don't release the tug toy back into play until he drops on cue.
- Don't let him sniff that fence until he gives you a 2-second eye contact.

These are not "rules to be strict." They're free reinforcers built into the environment.

## 8. Classical Counterconditioning — The Most Useful Tool For Fearful Dogs

If a dog already has a negative classical association with something (vet, dropped pan, men in hats, vacuum), you can rebuild it. Not erase — replace it with a stronger positive association.

The technique: present the trigger at *sub-threshold distance/intensity* + immediately produce extraordinary reinforcement. Repeat over many sessions. Slowly close the distance / raise the intensity. The dog's emotional response shifts.

**Critical:** the trigger always appears *before* the food. Trigger predicts food. If you flip the order (food then trigger), you accidentally signal that food predicts the scary thing → food becomes scary.

For Bucco: this is your tool if anything ever scares him. Vacuum, fireworks, a specific person — same protocol every time. Distance, food, repetition, patience.

## 9. The Distraction Intensity Index (DII) — Generalization Made Concrete

The single biggest training mistake: training a behavior at home, asking for it in the park, and getting frustrated when the dog "doesn't know it."

He doesn't know it. He knows it *at home.* Same behavior, new environment, new internal state — for the dog, it's almost a different cue.

Garrett's distraction scale (1-10):

| DII | Environment example | When to train here |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Living room, alone, no toys | Teaching a new behavior |
| 2 | Living room, Nina present | Early fluency |
| 3 | Hallway, mild distractions | Fluency check |
| 4 | Backyard, no other animals | Adding mild challenge |
| 5 | Backyard with squirrels visible | Real test of fluency |
| 6 | Driveway, occasional passing car | Building generalization |
| 7 | Quiet park, no other dogs | Recall begins to be testable |
| 8 | Sidewalk, other dogs at distance | Reliability check |
| 9 | Busy outdoor patio, kids, dogs | Late-stage proofing |
| 10 | Game-day chaos near the stadium | Master level |

**Rules:**

1. Train new things only at DII 1-2.
2. Generalize to a new DII only when 90% reliable at the previous level.
3. If you have to skip a level, go back and fill it in later.
4. **Don't test what you haven't trained.** Don't call your dog off a squirrel if you haven't trained recall at DII 5-6 first.

## 10. Why "No" Doesn't Work

Three biological reasons:

1. **It tells the dog what NOT to do, not what TO do.** The brain doesn't process negative space well. The dog needs a replacement behavior.
2. **It often triggers cortisol release** even at mild volume. Cortisol disrupts memory of the current event, so the dog doesn't remember what he was just punished for.
3. **It generalizes poorly.** "No" said over a chewed shoe means "don't chew shoes." It doesn't mean "don't chew anything." The dog learns context, not the broader rule.

**Better:**

- Manage the environment so the wrong behavior can't happen.
- Reinforce the right behavior heavily when it happens.
- Use Premack/IYC so the dog chooses the right thing because it pays better.

## 11. Extinction — When Behaviors Fade

If a previously-reinforced behavior stops being reinforced, it eventually fades. This sounds simple but two things complicate it:

### Extinction burst

The first response to extinction is *more* of the behavior, not less. The dog tries harder. If you don't reinforce a jumping greeting → he jumps harder for a session or two before the jumping subsides.

**You must outlast the extinction burst.** If you cave during it, you taught the dog that the behavior, when amplified, works.

### Spontaneous recovery

A behavior that's been extinguished can pop back up later, often after a break. Treat it the same — don't reinforce, wait for it to fade again.

## 12. The Garrett Mindset Rules (re-stated, science-anchored)

| Rule | Underlying science |
|---|---|
| "What you grow is what you get." | Operant conditioning — every interaction reinforces *something*. |
| Errorless learning. | Successive approximation — small steps prevent frustration, accelerate acquisition. |
| Recognize vs. know. | Generalization research — context-bound behavior ≠ true cue. |
| Classical conditioning before operant. | Pavlov + amygdala/hippocampus — emotional state gates operant accessibility. |
| Premack. | Behavioral economics — relative probability defines what's reinforcing. |
| No corrections. | Cortisol research — punishment impairs learning and damages relationship. |
| High rate of reinforcement. | Attention/dopamine — frequent positive predictions keep system engaged. |
| End on a win. | Recency effect — last experience anchors emotional memory of session. |

## 13. The Three Questions To Ask Before Any Training Decision

1. **What do I want him to do?** (Define the behavior, not "stop X.")
2. **What's reinforcing the unwanted behavior?** (Follow the money — every behavior pays in some currency.)
3. **How do I make the wanted behavior pay better than the alternative?** (Sometimes the answer is environmental management, not training.)

## 14. When Behaviors Don't Generalize — The Diagnostic

Bucco won't sit in the parking lot when he sits at home. What's actually going on?

| Possible cause | Test | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Behavior not actually fluent at home | Ask 10 times at home; succeed ≥9? | Train at lower DII until fluent |
| DII too high | Move 20 ft away from distraction | Drop DII, rebuild |
| Stress / arousal too high | Watch for panting, lip licks, scanning | Decompress first, train later |
| Hungry/tired/under-slept | Has he eaten? Slept enough? | Reschedule |
| Reinforcer not valuable enough | Use tier 3-4 treats here | Match treat value to DII |
| Cue contaminated | Have you been repeating the cue when he doesn't respond? | Quit using it; re-teach with new cue word |

## 15. The Single Most Useful Habit

**Capture, don't command.**

Default offered behaviors are stronger than cued ones, because the dog initiated them. Capturing means: notice the dog doing something good, mark, reinforce, move on. No cue.

- He sits when he sees the food bowl → mark + treat.
- He lies down because he's tired → mark + treat.
- He looks at you for no reason → mark + treat.
- He passes a cat without staring → mark + treat.

Within weeks, the dog is offering more of these behaviors *spontaneously*. They become defaults. Defaults are what hold up during adolescence.
