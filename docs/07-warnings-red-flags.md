# Warnings, Red Flags, and Things That Can't Be Walked Back

A focused document on what NOT to do, what to watch for, and when to get professional help. Some of this is breed-specific. Some of it applies to any puppy.

---

## 1. The Never List (Border Collie-specific)

These are things that, once done, are hard or impossible to undo.

### 1.1 Laser pointers

**Never. Not once. Not "just for a minute."**

BCs are the single most-susceptible breed to compulsive light fixation. There are documented cases of dogs developing lifetime light-chasing obsession from one play session. The fixation looks like fun at the time. Then it persists when the laser is off — staring at reflections, fan shadows, headlight patterns on the ceiling, light fluctuations from leaves. Years of veterinary behavioral intervention may be required.

There is no safe BC use case for laser pointers. There is no version of "I'll be careful." If a guest pulls one out, you take it.

### 1.2 Reflective surface play

Mirrors, polished metal, water reflections, screen reflections. If Bucco shows interest, redirect immediately to a higher-value activity. Don't laugh, don't engage. The first time is curiosity; the fifth time is a habit; the fiftieth is a pathology.

### 1.3 Dog parks

A dog park is an unstructured space with random dogs of varying social skills, varying training, and varying owner attentiveness. BCs are particularly poorly served by this environment for three reasons:

1. **They get bullied.** Smaller frame, intense focus, often reads as "weird" to dogs of less sensitive breeds. Easy target.
2. **They invent herding/control behaviors.** The BC who can't stop other dogs from running becomes the BC who tries to control them with nips and body-blocks. This generalizes to all dogs.
3. **They learn that other dogs are scary or chase-worthy.** Either outcome is bad and both are common.

Structured puppy class > dog park. Always. Forever. Bucco can be 4 years old and well-socialized and a dog park is still a bad idea for him.

### 1.4 Repetitive ball throwing as primary exercise

A BC will retrieve until physical collapse. The brain gets dopamine pulses from the chase phase, but never from a settle/end signal — so the dog spirals into hyper-arousal rather than tiring. You can produce a chronically anxious, obsessive ball-driven BC by this single training pattern.

Use fetch with structure: 5-10 retrieves, settle on cue, drink water, end on calm. Not 45 minutes of nonstop throwing.

### 1.5 Sustained running with no thinking

Marathon walks, treadmill running, bike-jogs without breaks. Tires the body, leaves the brain wired. Worse: it builds physical conditioning that then *demands* more activity for "tiredness." You'll be hiking 4 hours a day at year 3.

Instead: 20-min training session → calm settle → 30-min decompression walk → calm settle → puzzle feeder. The puppy is *more* tired with less physical exertion when the brain has been engaged.

## 2. The Never List (Universal Puppy)

### 2.1 Adult dog food during growth

Adult food doesn't guarantee the growth-phase calcium/phosphorus profile. Feeding it during growth can cause skeletal disorders.

### 2.2 Calcium, vitamin D, or vitamin A supplementation

Beyond what's in the food. All three cause harm in excess.

### 2.3 Cooked bones (any kind)

They splinter. Splinters perforate the esophagus, stomach, intestine. Raw meaty bones under supervision, after adult teeth are fully in (~6-7 months), is a different conversation — but never cooked.

### 2.4 Sudden food changes

Always 7-10 day transitions. The microbiome needs time.

### 2.5 The toxic-food list

| Food | Why |
|---|---|
| Grapes & raisins | Acute kidney failure, mechanism unknown, no safe dose |
| Onions, garlic, chives, leeks | Hemolytic anemia |
| Chocolate (especially dark) | Theobromine + caffeine toxicity |
| Xylitol | Insulin release → severe hypoglycemia (deadly) |
| Macadamia nuts | Neurological symptoms |
| Avocado | Persin (mostly an issue in large quantities) |
| Yeast dough | Ferments in stomach |
| Alcohol | Liver, kidney, neurological |
| Cooked bones | Perforation |
| Apple cores, peach pits, cherry pits | Cyanide compounds |
| Coffee, tea, energy drinks | Caffeine toxicity |
| Salt (large amounts) | Sodium ion poisoning |
| Pizza dough, raw bread dough | Yeast + alcohol fermentation |
| Marijuana | THC toxicity (and worsening with edibles) |

### 2.6 Stairs and furniture jumping under 6 months

Growth plates are open. A bad landing this month → orthopedic problem at year 3 or 4. Carry the puppy on stairs. Block jumps off the couch.

### 2.7 Forced exercise during growth

The 5-minute rule: 5 minutes of structured leash walking per month of age, up to twice a day. So an 8-month-old gets max 40-minute structured walks. Free play in safe spaces is different — the puppy self-regulates.

This rule is conservative; some studies don't show clean cause-effect at moderate exercise levels. But for a BC with a working drive that will *not* self-regulate, the rule is protective.

### 2.8 "Alpha rolling" or any dominance-based correction

Decades of research have eroded the dominance model of dog training. The "alpha wolf" framework Cesar Millan popularized was based on captive-wolf studies that were later debunked by the researcher who did them (David Mech, who literally wrote the book and has spent decades trying to retract his earlier work). Forcing a dog onto its back, pinning, or "showing dominance" is at best ineffective and at worst dangerous (you'll get bit).

### 2.9 Leash corrections (pops/jerks)

They suppress behavior in the immediate context without teaching what to do instead. They damage the human-dog relationship. They produce cortisol responses. There is no version of this that's appropriate for Bucco's training plan.

### 2.10 E-collars / shock collars / prong collars

Same logic as leash corrections plus equipment-specific damage. The training plan doesn't need them. Anyone telling you it does is selling you something or hasn't kept up with the science.

## 3. Behavioral Red Flags — Get Help Now, Not Later

These are behaviors that respond *dramatically* to early intervention (days to weeks) and very poorly to late intervention (months to years). Don't try to handle these alone.

### 3.1 Resource guarding

**Early signs (immediate intervention):**
- Stiffening over a food bowl, toy, chew
- Lip lift / showing teeth when approached over a valued item
- Quick gulping of food when someone walks near
- Possessive of a spot (couch, bed) with low growl when approached

**Why intervene fast:** resource guarding intensifies with every successful occurrence. By the time it's a bite, you're months into reconditioning.

**First aid:** trade-up protocol. Never just take things away. Always offer a higher-value swap. Walk by the food bowl during meals and drop in something amazing. Make humans approaching valued items a *prediction of more value*, not less.

**Get help:** if growl, bared teeth, snap, or bite has happened, call a KPA-CTP or CPDT-KA certified trainer — or a board-certified veterinary behaviorist if it's already at snap/bite stage.

### 3.2 Reactivity

**Early signs:**
- Barking and lunging at other dogs on leash
- Stiff posture, raised hackles, intense stare at triggers
- Difficulty disengaging from a stimulus
- "Trigger stacking" — calmer stimuli setting him off after a hard day

**Why intervene fast:** reactivity is self-reinforcing. The dog barks → the trigger goes away (because the dog walking past keeps walking) → the dog learned barking works.

**First aid:** distance. Never put him over threshold. If he's reacting, you're already too close. Cross the street, U-turn, create distance. Feed at sub-threshold distance to build a positive emotional response.

**Get help:** if it's persistent or escalating beyond 2-3 weeks of management.

### 3.3 Compulsive behaviors

**Early signs:**
- Staring at lights, shadows, reflections
- Tail chasing that doesn't stop on a redirect
- Snapping at invisible flies / air biting
- Flank sucking / self-licking that creates skin lesions
- Repetitive pacing in patterns

**Why intervene fast:** these can become neurological over months. The treatable window is short.

**First aid:** interrupt immediately and redirect to a high-engagement activity. Document frequency and context.

**Get help:** veterinary behaviorist NOW for any persistent compulsion. Penn Vet, NC State, Cornell, Purdue, and UC Davis all have behavior services that accept referrals.

### 3.4 Severe noise phobia

**Early signs:**
- Trembling, hiding, drooling at thunder/fireworks
- Refusing to eat for hours after a noise event
- Generalizing fear to weather changes (barometric pressure, wind)

**Why intervene fast:** sound phobias generalize and worsen with each exposure. The dog who fears thunder this year fears wind next year and stairs the year after.

**First aid:** sound desensitization with food during meals at LOW volume. Pair scary sounds with extraordinary reinforcement.

**Get help:** anti-anxiety medications (Sileo, trazodone, gabapentin) are available and well-tolerated; ask the vet. Behavior modification + medication together produces much better outcomes than either alone.

### 3.5 Bite incidents (not nipping)

A bite is a tooth-on-skin event with intent or force. Nipping during play with no bruise or break is normal puppy behavior. A bite that breaks skin, leaves bruising, or is delivered with intent (not play) is different.

**Get help immediately.** This is not a "wait and see." Document: who, what, when, where, what was the dog doing 60 seconds before, what was the human doing 60 seconds before.

### 3.6 Separation distress that doesn't resolve

Some puppy fussing on isolation is normal. *Persistent* distress that doesn't resolve with proper crate conditioning over 2-3 weeks is a different problem.

**Signs:**
- Salivating, panting, drooling when left alone
- Destruction focused on exit points (doors, windows)
- House-soiling only when alone
- Refusing to eat when alone

**Get help:** before it worsens. Separation anxiety has good treatment protocols, but they require expertise.

### 3.7 Sudden behavior change

A dog that was fine and now isn't. Sudden aggression, sudden fear, sudden lethargy, sudden change in appetite. **The first stop is the vet, not the trainer.** Pain, hormonal changes, neurological issues, and metabolic problems can all present as "behavior change."

## 4. Medical Red Flags — When To Call The Vet

### 4.1 Emergency (go now)

- Bloated, hard abdomen + non-productive retching (GDV / bloat — life-threatening, deep-chested breeds at higher risk but ANY dog can develop it)
- Suspected toxin ingestion (any of the toxic foods, medications, chemicals, certain plants)
- Heatstroke signs: heavy panting, drooling, weakness, collapse, body temp >104°F
- Hives, facial swelling, vomiting after a vaccine or insect sting (anaphylaxis)
- Sustained seizures or seizures lasting >2 minutes
- Significant trauma — hit by car, dropped, attacked
- Heavy or persistent bleeding
- Inability to urinate or defecate

### 4.2 Urgent (today)

- Vomiting more than 2-3× in a few hours
- Diarrhea with blood
- Refusing food for >24 hr (puppy under 6 months: <12 hr is concerning due to hypoglycemia risk)
- Significant lethargy out of character
- Limping that doesn't resolve in a few hours
- Painful abdomen on gentle touch
- Excessive water drinking (polyuria/polydipsia)

### 4.3 Soon (within a few days)

- Loose stool persisting >48 hr
- Coughing, sneezing more than occasional
- Skin issues (itching, hot spots, lesions)
- Ear shaking, head tilt, scratching at ears
- Eye discharge or squinting

### 4.4 At Bucco's age — extra-vigilant on

- **Parvo.** Even fully vaccinated puppies (until ~2 weeks post-final shot, ~late June 2026) are at residual risk. Signs: bloody diarrhea, vomiting, lethargy, refusal to eat. Mortality without treatment is very high. ER immediately if suspected.
- **Hypoglycemia.** Under 6 months, prolonged fasting can drop blood sugar dangerously fast. Honey or Karo syrup on the gums + immediate vet visit if you find him weak, wobbly, or unresponsive.
- **GI obstruction.** Puppies eat things. Socks, hair ties, rocks, toys. Vomiting + lethargy + abdominal pain = vet now, not tomorrow.

## 5. Vaccine Reactions

### 5.1 Normal post-vaccine (next 24-48 hrs)

- Mild lethargy / extra sleepiness
- Slight swelling at injection site
- Low-grade fever (you won't measure but you'll see a sleepier dog)
- Reduced appetite for one meal
- Slight soreness at injection site

### 5.2 Concerning (call the vet)

- Persistent vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Soft swelling at injection site persisting >5 days

### 5.3 Emergency (ER now)

- **Hives** — bumps under the coat anywhere on body
- **Facial swelling** — especially around eyes/muzzle
- **Vomiting + collapse / weakness**
- **Difficulty breathing**
- **Severe lethargy + pale gums**

For Bucco: the June 11 vaccine visit is a candidate for these. Watch the first 4 hours closely.

## 6. The Things That Look Bad But Usually Aren't

(So you don't panic at the wrong things.)

- **Hiccups.** Normal in puppies; they grow out of it.
- **"Frenetic Random Activity Period" (zoomies).** The 8pm puppy explosion is normal energy release. If it always happens at the same time of day and resolves in 5-10 minutes, it's fine.
- **Stinky breath in a teething puppy.** Adult teeth coming in disrupt the gum line. Some smell is normal. Persistent rotting-fish smell or visible gum disease is not.
- **One or two soft poops in a busy week.** Diet variation, mild stress, or just normal variability. Persistent loose stool >48 hr is different.
- **Reverse sneezing** (a honking, snorting inhalation). Looks alarming. Almost always benign. Gently cover one nostril for a few seconds to break the spasm.
- **Slight injection site swelling.** Normal up to several days.
- **Mild "puppy regression"** post-vaccine (3-5 days of clumsier training). Normal cortisol response to immune activation.
- **A single skipped meal** in a 12-week-old. Watch the next meal. If two in a row, call the vet.

## 7. The "Right Person For Help" Decision Tree

```
Problem →
  Medical? → VETERINARIAN
  Behavioral but mild and recent? → KPA-CTP or CPDT-KA certified trainer using +R methods
  Behavioral with bite history? → VETERINARY BEHAVIORIST (DACVB)
  Behavioral + suspected medical component? → VET FIRST, then DACVB or trainer
  Sport-specific (agility, herding)? → Sport-specific instructor with credentials in the sport
  Compulsive behavior? → DACVB (don't pass go, don't try anything else first)
```

## 8. The Single Most Important Sentence

**Early intervention works much better than late intervention for behavioral problems. Trust your gut. If something seems off, get an opinion before it becomes a pattern.**

The cost of an early trainer consult: $100-200 and an evening.

The cost of a late veterinary behaviorist consult after a serious bite incident: $400-1000 + 6-12 months of remediation + a relationship and home that may never feel the same.

Cheap on the front end. Expensive on the back end. The math is unambiguous.

## 9. The Things The Internet Will Tell You That Are Wrong

- "BCs need 4 hours of exercise a day or they'll go crazy." Mental work beats physical work. 20 min of training tires a BC more than 2 hours of running. Both have value, but the training is the more sustainable answer.

- "He'll grow out of it." Most behavioral issues *intensify* through adolescence, not improve. If you'd consider it a problem at 3 months, it'll be a bigger problem at 18 months. Address it now.

- "Just dominate him; show him who's boss." See section 2.8. The dominance model is debunked. The handlers who insist on it loudest are usually the ones with the worst-behaved dogs.

- "Stop using treats; he should obey because he respects you." Behavioral science is unambiguous: behavior persists when reinforced. Withhold reinforcement and behavior fades. Treats are the most efficient and humane reinforcer for most situations. They aren't a crutch; they're the technology.

- "Mix some adult food in early to teach his digestion." No. Adult food doesn't have the growth-phase nutrient profile. Stick to a good puppy or all-life-stages-with-growth food.

- "Walk him for an hour twice a day or he'll be hyperactive." Puppy under 6 months: don't do this. See section 2.7.

- "Let other dogs correct him; he needs to learn." Letting an unknown dog "correct" your puppy is a great way to give him a lifetime fear of dogs. The "correction" might be appropriate or might be an over-the-top aggressive response from a poorly-socialized adult. You don't get to choose; you only see the outcome.

## 10. The Last Warning

**The biggest risk to Bucco isn't disease, isn't injury, isn't bad genetics. It's accumulated tiny errors that compound over 18 months of adolescence.**

A leash pop here. A laser pointer "just for fun" there. A skipped socialization week. A few extra calories that build into a 15% overweight 1-year-old. A reactivity event left unaddressed. None of these is catastrophic alone. All of them compound.

The compounding works in the other direction too. A handful of right choices every day, repeated daily, builds a dog who is fundamentally different at 18 months from one whose handler made a handful of wrong choices.

**Compound interest applies to puppy training.**

That's it.
