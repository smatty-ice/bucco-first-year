# Feeding Bucco — The Real Science Behind The Numbers

What goes in matters more than most owners realize. For a growing medium-breed working dog, "good enough" food + slightly off macros = orthopedic problems at year three. This document walks through what to feed, how much, why, and where the science actually points.

---

## 1. Caloric Requirements — The Math, Properly

### Resting Energy Requirement (RER)

The baseline metabolic burn. Every dog, regardless of activity:

> **RER (kcal/day) = 70 × (body weight in kg)^0.75**

This is the Kleiber equation, the empirical metabolic-scaling law that holds across mammals from mice to elephants. The exponent (0.75) reflects that smaller bodies burn proportionally more per gram than larger ones.

### Maintenance Energy Requirement (MER)

RER multiplied by a lifestyle factor:

| Lifestyle/Stage | Multiplier | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Puppy 0-4 months | **×3.0** | Highest of any life stage |
| Puppy 4-12 months | **×2.0** | Growth slows |
| Adult intact, active | ×1.6 | Working BCs often run here |
| Adult intact, moderate | ×1.4 | Pet dog standard |
| Adult neutered, light | ×1.2-1.4 | Neutering reduces metabolism ~25% |
| Senior (7+) | ×1.2 | |
| Working dog (active) | ×2-5 | Sled dogs hit ×8 in winter |

### Bucco's current target (week 12, ~12-18 lb)

| Weight | kg | RER | ×3 (kcal/day) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 lb | 5.4 | 248 | **~745** |
| 14 lb | 6.4 | 282 | **~845** |
| 16 lb | 7.3 | 312 | **~935** |
| 18 lb | 8.2 | 343 | **~1030** |

### When the multiplier drops to ×2 (around 4-5 months)

| Weight | kg | RER | ×2 (kcal/day) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 20 lb | 9.1 | 369 | **~740** |
| 25 lb | 11.3 | 437 | **~875** |
| 30 lb | 13.6 | 501 | **~1000** |
| 35 lb | 15.9 | 564 | **~1130** |
| 40 lb | 18.1 | 622 | **~1245** |

You'll notice calorie target stops climbing rapidly once the multiplier drops. This is correct. **Growth slows; caloric need plateaus; weight continues climbing on the same calorie budget because activity increases too.**

## 2. Macronutrients — What the Calories Should Be

Per AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials) puppy growth standards, dry matter basis:

| Nutrient | Minimum | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | 22.5% | Quality matters: animal-based > plant-based amino acid profiles |
| **Fat** | 8.5% | Critical for brain development; DHA omega-3 is the big one |
| **Calcium** | 1.0% | Maximum 1.8% for large-breed growth |
| **Phosphorus** | 0.8% | |
| **Ca:P ratio** | 1.1:1 to 1.3:1 | This ratio matters more than absolute calcium |

### Protein: How much, what kind?

Old myth: "high protein causes orthopedic problems." Demolished by Nap & Hazewinkel's 1991 study in the *Journal of Nutrition* — Great Dane puppies fed 14.6%, 23.1%, or 31.6% protein diets showed **no differences in skeletal development.** Protein was never the culprit.

What does protein quality mean? Amino acid profile. Dogs require 10 essential amino acids: arginine, histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine. Animal proteins (meat, eggs, dairy) have all 10 in good ratios. Plant proteins are usually deficient in 1-3 — so a chicken-and-rice food generally beats a pea-and-rice food on amino acid availability.

### Fat: The brain fuel

Brain dry matter is ~60% fat. The growing puppy brain needs DHA (docosahexaenoic acid, a long-chain omega-3) for membrane and myelin formation. Studies (Zicker et al., Hill's research) have shown puppies fed DHA-enhanced diets score better on visual and cognitive learning tasks.

Look for: fish oil, salmon, algae oil in the ingredients. Minimum DHA: 0.05% dry matter for growing puppies.

### Carbs: Not the enemy, not required

Dogs have no minimum carbohydrate requirement. They can derive glucose from protein via gluconeogenesis. **But** carbs are a cheap energy source and most kibbles use them. There's nothing wrong with a moderate-carb diet for a non-working pet dog. Grain-inclusive vs. grain-free is largely marketing (see DCM discussion below).

## 3. Calcium, Phosphorus, and The Single Most Important Number

**This is the most important macro for a large/medium-breed puppy.** Get it wrong and you're looking at hip dysplasia, OCD (osteochondrosis), angular limb deformities, or compression fractures.

Hazewinkel's seminal 1985-1991 work at Utrecht demonstrated:

- Excess dietary calcium in growing large-breed puppies causes osteochondrosis, delayed endochondral ossification, and angular limb deformities — **independent of total energy intake**.
- A 2017 case report (PMC 5731398) documented a large-breed puppy with compression fractures, vitamin D deficiency, and suspected nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism from an unbalanced homemade diet.

### What to look for on the bag

- **AAFCO statement:** must say "complete and balanced for growth" or "for all life stages including growth of large size dogs (70 lb or more as an adult)." For Bucco (medium breed, ~35-50 lb adult), the "growth" statement is sufficient — he's not large enough to require the large-breed-specific statement, but a food formulated for it is fine.
- **Calcium:** 1.0-1.8% dry matter for growing puppies. Lower end (1.0-1.4%) is safer for medium/large.
- **Phosphorus:** 0.8-1.6%.
- **Ca:P ratio:** 1.1:1 to 1.3:1 is ideal. Above 1.6:1 or below 1.0:1 is concerning.

### What NOT to do

- Don't supplement calcium "to help his bones grow." This is the most common harmful mistake. The calcium in his food is what his bones need; more is harmful, not better.
- Don't feed adult dog food during growth. Adult formulas don't guarantee the calcium/phosphorus profile he needs.
- Don't switch foods every month. Sudden GI disruption + nutrient profile changes during growth = trouble.

## 4. The Grain-Free / DCM Story

In 2018, the FDA opened an investigation into a possible link between grain-free diets (specifically those high in peas, lentils, legumes, potatoes) and **dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM)** in breeds not historically prone to it.

State of the science as of late 2024 / 2025:

- **No causative link has been definitively established.** The FDA's investigation found a statistical correlation but couldn't isolate a single mechanism.
- One hypothesis: taurine deficiency related to the legume-heavy formulations. Some affected dogs had low blood taurine; many didn't.
- Many initially-affected dogs improved on a diet change, suggesting *something* dietary is involved.
- The FDA narrowed its focus to specific brand families with high legume content and "boutique" formulations from smaller manufacturers.

**Practical recommendation:** Without a documented grain allergy or sensitivity, default to a grain-inclusive food from a manufacturer with veterinary nutritionists on staff and a long track record. Brands meeting the "WSAVA Global Nutrition Committee" recommended criteria include Royal Canin, Hill's, Purina Pro Plan, and Eukanuba. (This is not an endorsement; it's a risk-reduction default.)

If Bucco has confirmed grain allergy: grain-free is fine, but pick one with limited legume content and animal protein as #1 ingredient.

## 5. Meal Frequency

| Age | Meals per day | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 8-16 weeks | **4** | Small stomach, fast metabolism, hypoglycemia risk |
| 4-6 months | **3** | Stomach capacity increases |
| 6-12 months | **2-3** | Growth slowing |
| 12+ months | **2** | Adult maintenance |

**Why not free-feed?** Three reasons:

1. *Lost training currency.* If food is always available, food is no longer reinforcing.
2. *Obscured appetite as health signal.* A skipped meal is one of the earliest signs of illness; you won't notice if you don't track.
3. *Resource-guarding prevention.* Food has structure and ritual; dogs don't feel scarce around it.

## 6. Treats as a Training Tool

### Hierarchy of value (Bucco-specific, build with his preferences)

- **Tier 1 (lowest):** his regular kibble. Sufficient at home, low distraction.
- **Tier 2:** training treats, freeze-dried liver, small cheese cubes, baby carrots.
- **Tier 3:** boiled chicken breast, hot dog pieces (sodium! sparingly), the occasional small piece of *plain* scrambled egg.
- **Tier 4 (rare):** something he loses his mind over. For Bucco, this might be a specific cheese, real liver, or a high-value tug toy.

Match value to difficulty. Recall in the backyard? Kibble might do. Recall away from a squirrel? Tier 3-4.

### The 10% rule (or 20% if using kibble)

If treats are *separate* from his daily food: total treat calories ≤ 10% of daily calories.

If you're using his daily kibble *as* training treats (the Garrett default at home): up to ~80% of his daily food can be delivered through training. The total stays the same; the delivery method shifts.

### Treat math example

Bucco at 15 lb, week 12, 880 kcal/day target.

- 10% treat cap (if separate): ≤88 kcal in treats.
- Typical training treat = 2-3 kcal. That's ~30-40 small treats *of separate treats* per day.
- If kibble: ~ ½ cup kibble = ~200 kcal = ~100-150 individual pieces of kibble.

### What NOT to give

Toxic to dogs, full stop:

- **Grapes, raisins** — acute kidney failure, mechanism still poorly understood
- **Onions, garlic, leeks** — hemolytic anemia
- **Chocolate** — theobromine and caffeine toxicity (darker = worse)
- **Xylitol** — sweetener in sugar-free gum, peanut butter, mints; causes life-threatening hypoglycemia
- **Macadamia nuts** — neurological symptoms
- **Alcohol** — fatal
- **Cooked bones** — splinter, perforate GI tract
- **Avocado** — persin, mostly an issue in large quantities
- **Yeast dough** — ferments in stomach
- **Apple cores / stone fruit pits** — cyanide compounds

## 7. The Body Condition Score (BCS) — The Real Metric

Numbers on a scale are imprecise (a kibble swing, a recent meal, a recent pee all shift it). BCS is the standard.

**9-point scale (Purina / WSAVA):**

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Emaciated. Ribs, vertebrae, pelvic bones obvious from a distance. No fat. |
| 2 | Very thin. Bones clearly visible, minimal flesh. |
| 3 | Thin. Ribs easily palpable, slight fat covering. Tops of vertebrae visible. |
| 4 | Underweight. Ribs easily palpable with minimal fat. Visible waist. |
| **5** | **Ideal. Ribs palpable without excess fat. Waist visible from above. Tummy tuck from side.** |
| **6** | **Slightly overweight but acceptable for many BCs.** |
| 7 | Overweight. Ribs palpable with effort, fat covering. Waist hard to see. |
| 8 | Obese. Ribs hard to find. Pendulous belly. No waist. |
| 9 | Severely obese. Massive fat deposits. |

**For a growing BC, target BCS 4-5.** Lean wins for joint health and longevity (Purina's landmark 14-year lifespan study on Labradors found lean dogs lived ~1.8 years longer on average).

**Recheck every 2 weeks during growth.** Adjust by ±10% on the calorie target.

## 8. Water

A growing puppy needs ~½ to 1 oz per pound of body weight per day. For Bucco at 15 lb: roughly 7-15 oz/day. More on hot days, more after exercise.

Provide fresh water at all times. Bowl: stainless steel or ceramic (plastic harbors bacteria, can cause chin acne). Wash daily.

## 9. Supplements: What Bucco Actually Needs

If you're feeding a complete and balanced food meeting AAFCO growth standards, **the answer is almost always: nothing.** But:

- **Fish oil** — extra omega-3 (DHA/EPA) for brain development and skin. 1000mg fish oil per ~10 lb body weight, split daily. Pick a brand with third-party testing for mercury and PCBs.
- **Probiotics** — *if* GI issues are recurring. Otherwise marginal. Specific strains with research: Bifidobacterium longum, Lactobacillus plantarum.
- **Joint supplements (glucosamine, chondroitin)** — research is mixed for healthy young dogs. Most veterinary nutritionists don't recommend in puppyhood.
- **NEVER** supplement calcium, vitamin D, or vitamin A. All three can cause harm in excess.

## 10. Food Transitions

Every change should happen over 7-10 days. Sudden changes = osmotic diarrhea (water rushes into the gut to dilute the unfamiliar contents).

| Day | Old food | New food |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | 75% | 25% |
| 3-4 | 50% | 50% |
| 5-6 | 25% | 75% |
| 7+ | 0% | 100% |

Watch stool quality. Slight loosening for 2-3 days is normal. Frank diarrhea, vomiting, or blood = slow the transition or back off entirely.

## 11. The Microbiome Angle

Newer research (covered in the neuroscience chapter) links gut microbiome composition to canine anxiety and aggression. The actionable items for Bucco:

- **Feeding consistency over feeding novelty.** Frequent food changes disrupt the microbiome.
- **Limit antibiotic exposure** to when medically required.
- **Prebiotic fibers in the food** support beneficial gut bacteria. Look for: chicory root, beet pulp, FOS (fructooligosaccharides).
- **A spoon of plain unsweetened yogurt or kefir** a few times a week is well-tolerated by most dogs and provides probiotic strains. *Skip if any lactose sensitivity.*

## 12. The Susan Garrett "Train With Meals" Philosophy — Why It's Sound

Bucco gets ~80% of his daily food *through training*, not from a bowl.

Why this works:

1. **Engagement.** Food from the handler builds the handler as the source of good things.
2. **Reinforcement budget.** A puppy eats 4× per day. Each meal becomes 100+ training reps. That's 400+ reps/day at no additional caloric cost.
3. **Calm meal patterns.** Eating quietly from a hand, a mat, a Kong, or a snuffle mat trains arousal regulation around food.
4. **Default behaviors.** "When food appears, what do you offer?" becomes a question the puppy starts answering proactively.

What the remaining ~20% looks like: frozen Kongs, lick mats, snuffle mats, food-dispensing toys — all enrichment, all building independent calm play.
